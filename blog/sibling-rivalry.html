How to handle sibling rivalry?

"That's not fair!", "You love them more than me!", "They got more than I did!"… Sibling rivalry is a reality in almost every family. Far from being a sign of parenting failure, it's a completely normal emotion — and a chance to learn how to live together.

💛 Why sibling jealousy is normal

Every child longs to be loved in a unique and exclusive way. The arrival of a sibling, or simply the feeling that the other child is getting more attention, triggers an emotional competition rooted in a deep need for belonging and security.

It's not a character flaw — it's biology and development. Sibling conflicts are, in the long run, an excellent school for life: they teach negotiation, empathy, and conflict resolution.

💡 Key takeaway: your goal isn't to eliminate jealousy, but to help your children express it without hurting each other — and to find their own place within the family.

🎯 1. Avoid comparisons — even positive ones

"Look how nicely your sister tidied her room" sounds harmless, but immediately sets the children in competition. Every comparison, even a flattering one for one child, feels like a put-down to the other.

Instead, use phrases that speak to each child about themselves: "You tidied your room really well — I'm proud of you" — with no reference to their sibling. Every child needs to be seen for who they are, not measured against someone else.

  • ❌ "Your brother eats without any fuss"
  • ✅ "I can see this meal is tricky for you tonight"
  • ❌ "Your sister finished her homework in 20 minutes"
  • ✅ "You worked hard, even though it took a while"

⏱️ 2. Give each child individual time

Jealousy often grows from a feeling of lack — not necessarily a lack of love, but of exclusive time with you. A child who regularly gets one-on-one time with a parent feels less in competition with their siblings.

It doesn't have to be long: 15 to 20 minutes of an activity chosen by the child, without the other, can make a real difference. A board game, a walk, a bedtime story — what matters is the exclusivity of the moment.

💡 Practical idea: set up a rotating "Saturday date" where each child picks an activity to do alone with mum or dad. Children look forward to their turn — and become less jealous of each other's!

🤝 3. Don't referee every fight — let them learn to resolve it

It's tempting to play judge at every dispute. But by stepping in every time, you deprive your children of the chance to learn to negotiate on their own.

Unless there's physical aggression or real distress, try letting them find a solution together. Ask questions rather than giving answers: "What could you both do to make this work for everyone?" Your role is to facilitate, not to decide.

In heated moments, calmly separate the children so everyone can cool down, then come back together to hear both sides — without looking for who's right or wrong.

🌟 4. Value the sibling relationship, not just the individuals

Children learn to value their sibling bond by watching their parents value it. Name the good moments: "You played so well together this morning — it was lovely to see." Create rituals that belong to the siblings — an activity reserved for "the big kids", a game you all play together, a family in-joke.

In the long run, the sibling bond is one of the most lasting relationships there is. Helping your children build it today is one of the greatest gifts you can give them.
