Baby food at 6 months: where to start?

Starting solid foods is a big milestone — for your baby and for you. New textures, new flavours, new reflexes to learn… No need to stress: it's an adventure that unfolds at its own pace, with curiosity and patience.

🌱 Why 6 months is the right time

The World Health Organization recommends starting solid foods around 6 months — no earlier than 4 months, and ideally no later than 7 months. By this age, your baby's digestive system is mature enough to handle more than milk, and several signs will tell you they're ready.

What to look for: they can hold their head steady, they show interest in what you're eating, they open their mouth when a spoon comes close, and the tongue-thrust reflex has faded (they no longer automatically push everything out of their mouth).

💡 Key takeaway: solids don't replace milk — they complement it. Until 12 months, breast milk or infant formula remains the foundation of your baby's diet.

🥕 1. Start simple: one vegetable at a time

There's no need for elaborate recipes on day one. The goal is discovery: introduce one food at a time over several days, to watch for any reactions (rashes, digestive upset) and give your baby time to get used to each new taste.

Mild vegetables are perfect for starting out: courgette, carrot, parsnip, sweet potato, green beans. Blended into a smooth purée, with no added salt, served lukewarm.

  • 🟠 Carrot — sweet, gentle, very well accepted
  • 🟡 Sweet potato — soft and naturally sweet
  • 🟢 Courgette — light and easy to digest
  • ⚪ Parsnip — delicate flavour, creamy texture when blended

🥄 2. Match textures to your baby's age

Texture matters as much as what's in the bowl. A 6-month-old doesn't yet have the tools to chew — they work mostly with their tongue and palate. Smooth, lump-free purées are essential to avoid any choking risk.

Around 7–8 months, you can gradually thicken textures and introduce small soft pieces. By 9–10 months, tender chunks and soft finger foods become possible. Let your baby set the pace.

💡 Golden rule: never leave your baby to eat alone — always stay close and attentive during meals.

🍽️ 3. Make mealtimes calm and unhurried

The atmosphere around mealtimes matters as much as what's on the spoon. A relaxed baby, sitting comfortably, without distractions (no screens, no loud background noise) is far more willing to explore new tastes.

Offer solid meals at lunchtime first — your baby is rested, in a good mood, and if there's a reaction to a new food, you have the whole day to keep an eye on things. Never force it: if your baby turns their head away or clamps their mouth shut, respect that signal. It can take 10 to 15 tries before a food is accepted — that's completely normal.

🚫 4. Foods to avoid before 12 months

Some foods are firmly off the menu before 12 months for safety and health reasons:

  • 🍯 Honey — risk of infant botulism, avoid until after 1 year
  • 🥛 Cow's milk as a drink — the kidneys aren't ready for it yet
  • 🧂 Added salt and sugar — your baby's kidneys and palate simply don't need them
  • 🐟 Raw fish and processed meats — bacterial risk
  • 🥜 Whole nuts — choking hazard

💡 Good news: major allergens (egg, peanut, fish…) can be introduced from 6 months, in small quantities and one at a time — early introduction actually reduces the risk of allergy. Ask your paediatrician for guidance if your baby has a family history of allergies.

Weaning is an adventure

Your baby will grimace, spit things out, and smear purée everywhere with their fingers — that's completely normal and even necessary. Exploring food with all their senses is part of learning. Don't aim for a perfect meal every time: aim for curiosity and good spirits.

If you have any doubts about quantities, allergies, or timing, your paediatrician is always the best person to ask. And between appointments, you're not alone.
