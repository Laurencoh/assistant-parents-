My child won't eat: practical tips

The plate going cold, the crossed arms, the relentless "I don't like it"… Feeding difficulties affect most families at some point. The good news: in most cases, this isn't a medical problem — it's a phase. And there are real, concrete things you can do to get through it.

🍽️ Understanding why a child refuses to eat

Before looking for solutions, it's worth understanding what's actually going on. A child who refuses to eat isn't always being difficult: they might be tired, not hungry, sensitive to certain textures or new flavours, or simply exercising the only power they have — deciding what goes into their own body.

Food neophobia — the fear of new foods — is especially common between ages 2 and 6. It's not whimsy: it's an instinctive protective response that naturally decreases with age and repeated exposure to foods.

💡 Key takeaway: your role as a parent is to offer — not to force. It's your child's job to decide how much they eat. This division of responsibility dramatically reduces tension around mealtimes.

😌 1. Take the pressure off mealtimes

The more you push a child to eat, the more they resist. Pressure — even well-meaning pressure — turns mealtimes into a power struggle you can't win.

Try the opposite: make the meal neutral and pleasant. No comments on what they eat or don't eat. No negotiations ("eat two bites and you can have dessert"). No worried look when the plate stays full. Your calm at the table is the first ingredient of a successful meal.

  • 🚫 "Come on, one more bite for Mummy"
  • 🚫 "If you don't eat, you'll get sick"
  • ✅ "We're eating together — take what you like"
  • ✅ Moving on without drama if the child refuses

👨‍🍳 2. Get your child involved in cooking

A child who helped prepare a meal is far more curious to taste it. Even at 2, they can rinse vegetables, stir a sauce, or place ingredients in a bowl. It's not magic — it's ownership.

Take them food shopping and let them pick a vegetable or fruit they don't know. Having chosen something creates natural curiosity. Name what you're cooking together, explain where the food comes from — the meal starts long before the table.

💡 Easy idea: offer a "discovery plate" once a week — a few small portions of new or disliked things, presented nicely. No obligation to eat, just to explore.

🔄 3. Present foods differently

Sometimes it's not the food a child is rejecting — it's its shape, texture, or context. A child who refuses cooked carrots might love them raw and grated. One who hates fish fillets might enjoy homemade fish cakes or nuggets.

The 10 to 15 exposures rule is real: it can take many tries before a food is accepted. Exposure alone counts — the child doesn't have to eat it for it to "count". Seeing the food on the plate, touching it, smelling it — that's already progress.

🌙 4. Check the context: hunger, tiredness, routine

A child who's been snacking all afternoon won't be hungry at dinner. An exhausted child eats less well. A meal served very late throws off their natural rhythm. Before worrying about what's on the plate, check the context around it.

  • ⏰ Regular mealtimes that respect your child's natural rhythm
  • 🍎 No snack too close to a meal (at least 2 hours between)
  • 📵 No screens at the table — distraction reduces awareness of hunger
  • 😴 A rested child always eats better than an overstimulated one

💡 Reassurance: if your child is growing normally, active and healthy, they are eating enough — even if their plate often looks too empty to you. Children regulate their hunger better than we think.

When to see a doctor

If your child is losing weight, refusing food to the point of exhaustion, or showing intense anxiety around mealtimes, talk to your paediatrician. In the vast majority of cases, feeding difficulties resolve with time, patience — and a lot less pressure.
