My baby won't sleep at night: what to do?

Sleepless nights are among the most exhausting challenges of early parenthood. If your baby wakes up every hour, fights sleep, or cries the moment you put them down, know that you're not alone — and that things can get better.

1. Understanding your baby's sleep cycles

Unlike adults, newborns have very short sleep cycles — around 45 to 50 minutes — and spend far more time in light sleep. At the end of each cycle, they naturally surface and can wake up fully if something has changed (like being moved from your arms to the crib).

This is called a sleep association: if your baby falls asleep nursing or being held, they'll unconsciously seek those same conditions every time they stir.

💡 Key takeaway: put your baby down when they're drowsy but still awake. This gradually teaches them to fall asleep independently.

2. Building a consistent bedtime routine

A baby's brain responds well to repeated cues. A predictable routine helps their nervous system wind down and prepare for sleep. It doesn't need to be long — 20 to 30 minutes is plenty:

  1. Warm bath (optional, but very effective)
  2. Feed in a calm, quiet environment
  3. Gentle massage or cuddle in a dimly lit room
  4. Lullaby or soft voice
  5. Into the crib, drowsy

Consistency is everything: after a few days, your baby will start associating these steps with the coming night and fall asleep more easily.

3. Setting up a sleep-friendly environment

The environment plays a bigger role than most parents realise. A few simple tweaks can make a real difference:

  • Darkness: blackout curtains block the light cues that stimulate the brain
  • White noise: a fan, a dedicated app, or even a vacuum cleaner in the background — it mimics the sounds of the womb
  • Temperature: 65–68 °F (18–20 °C) is the sweet spot for infant sleep
  • Safe sleep sack: a wearable blanket keeps them at a steady temperature without the risk of loose bedding

4. Catching the sleep window

Every baby sends tiredness signals before they hit "overtired" territory. If you miss that window, cortisol kicks in and your baby becomes paradoxically harder to settle.

Watch for: eye-rubbing, glassy stare, yawning, slowing movements. The moment you spot them, start the routine — even if the time feels early.

💡 Tip: keep a short log of wake-up and sleep times for 3 days. A natural rhythm will quickly emerge — one you can lean into rather than fight.

When nothing seems to work

Some babies are simply more alert by temperament — that's completely normal, and it does get easier with age. If sleep disruptions are intense or have been going on for several weeks, bring it up with your pediatrician. They can rule out a medical cause (reflux, ear infections) and point you in the right direction.
